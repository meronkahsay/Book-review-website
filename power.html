THE POWER OF HABIT

Why we do what we do and how to change

Independence

“My mom did what school didn't. She taught me how to think.”

Trevor was raised by his mom as if there were no limitations on where he could go or what he could do.
She taught him to speak up and stand up for himself by leading the life he believed in and not what
is dictated by society. His mom taught him to claim his rights despite the situation and that his ideas,
thoughts and decisions mattered.
In African homes most firstborns learn the art of independence without them realizing it. Their parents
impose upon them responsibilities and tasks which teach them to be independent as they have to maneuver
and figure out things for themselves, though they get the support and guidance indirectly from their parents.

Rich without money

“As modestly as we lived at home, I never felt poor because our lives were so rich with experience.”
“Why teach a black child white things? 'Why do this? Why show him the world when he's never going to leave the ghetto?'
'Even if he never leaves the ghetto, he will know that the ghetto is not the world. If that is all I accomplish, I've done enough.”

Trevor's life was full of exploration as his mother refused to be bound by the rules that stated what black people couldn't
or shouldn't do. Patricia would always find places where she didn't have to spend money. She would take him to the ice rink
to go skating, to the drive-in movie theater, and taught him to drive around the white neighbourhood, which was always restricted.
Parents greatly influence and shape the reality that their children exist in. Unknowingly, the decisions made by our parents,
like sending us to good schools gave us a chance to experience and explore most of the things we wouldn't have experienced if
not in those good schools, like going on school trips, participating in different clubs, e.g Presidential Awards Club, where
we got to visit the Statehouse, STEM projects, and contests. These experiences filled our lives with so many adventures that
made us forget our financial situations.

Power of language

“Language brings with it an identity and a culture, or at least the perception of it. A shared language says, "We're the same."
A language barrier says, "We're different.

Trevor was always treated differently because of his color. He discovered the greatest method to overcome
his racial discrimination was through language, which defines who you are to people more than race.
Speaking someone else's language even basic phrases, shows that you understand their culture and identity
that is beyond you. When you visit a new place and you're familiar with their language, it creates a certain
bond and rapport, which makes it easy to communicate and understand each other.

Relationships

“The world doesn’t love you. If the police get you, the police don’t love you. When I beat you,
I’m trying to save you. When they beat you, they’re trying to kill you.”

Trevor's relationship with his mom was full of mutual respect and understanding.Growing up, Trevor did
not understand the tough relationship with his mother, but growing older, he started understanding that
his mother was beating him “out of love” before the system did.
As kids, being beaten by our parents was a daily norm. At the time, we thought it was out of hate, even
though they kept saying they were disciplining us out of love and to make us better people in society.
