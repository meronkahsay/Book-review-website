THE POWER OF HABIT

Why we do what we do and how to change

Habit loop

"As the route became more automatic, each rat started thinking less and less."

Duhigg explains the formation of habits using a framework of the cue, routine, and reward.
The Cue: Anything that initiates a habit. It can be time, location, emotional state, or even
a previous action. Just like someone who, whenever they feel angry or stressed, that’s a cue
for them to drink alcohol.
The Routine: The habit or behavior triggered by the cue. With the above example, drinking
alcohol can be a habit. And going for lunch at noon is a routine. A routine is the action that
is done automatically with no effort when a cue is identified.
The Reward: The outcome or the prize you get after your routine is the reward. For example,
if you drink alcohol, then your stress is relieved, or using perfume because you want to feel
the smell of fruits or any preferred scent.

The Craving Brain and the
golden rule of habit change

"He created a craving. And that craving , it turns out is what makes cues and rewards work."

The principle of the craving brain mainly outlines that when something becomes a habit,
the brain starts getting used to it to the extent that whenever a cue is identified, the brain
anticipates for the reward even before the routine is done, which makes the habit easier
to do without giving it thought because the brain is already craving for the reward. An
example is when you get to know that today chicken will be served, you’re already
feeling the sweetness of eating the chicken before you are even sure that it’s going to
be served. When everything becomes usual, the brain already knows what it will look
like before you perform the routine.
The golden rule of habit change suggests keeping the cue and reward and simply
changing the routine, since it creates a habit itself. For example, if you tend to reach
for a snack when you feel stressed, you can keep the stress as the cue and the feeling
of reward (e.g., relief, satisfaction) as the reward, but substitute a glass of water or
a walk for the snacks.

Keystone Habits

"As Alcoa's safety patterns shifted, other aspects of the company started changing
with startling speed as well."

Keystone habits are habits that not only affect one area of your life but also lay as a
cornerstone for other good habits. They influence your overall habits for the better good,
leading to significant improvements in various aspects of your life. Such habits include
reading, reading is a habit that will open you to various topics and worlds, hence altering
your perspective about life, which can change how you approach life. Starting to read as
a habit can lead to numerous other habits, like saving, physical activity, how you speak to
people, etc, all because you got lost in a book and started applying the dynamics of it.

The neurology of free will

"If you believe you can change, if you make it a habit, the change becomes real."

Charles Duhigg explores the neurology of free will, arguing that while habits can be
deeply ingrained, they are not destiny and can be changed. He suggests that individuals
can control their habits through conscious effort and understanding. Duhigg argues that
individuals are morally responsible for their habits because they can, through conscious
choice, change them. He emphasizes the concept, suggesting that people have the power
to choose how they act, even if habits have a strong influence. Duhigg emphasizes the
importance of believing in one's ability to change and make it a habit, as this belief is
crucial for achieving lasting change. Duhigg later concludes that habits are not destiny,
but rather that individuals have the power to choose their habits and create a
desired future. In other words, we constantly choose our habits by what actions we
perform and hold the power to change our habits to what we
want them to be by believing we can and making the steps towards our desired habits.
